## Definition

Gross collection rate is payments ÷ gross charges: what you collected as a share of what you billed at full fee-schedule rates. A practice billing at 250% of Medicare rates and collecting typical contracted amounts might show a gross rate near 40% — while collecting essentially everything it was entitled to.

## What It Actually Measures

Mostly, your fee schedule: raise charges and the rate falls with zero change in performance; lower charges and it rises the same way. Between two practices, gross rate differences reflect markup policies more than billing quality — which is why using it as a performance grade misleads, and why net collection rate exists.

## Where It Still Earns a Place

- **Trend monitoring on a stable fee schedule:** if charges have not changed, a falling gross rate flags something real — payer mix shift, rising denials, or collection decay — worth investigating
- **Payer mix visibility:** computed per payer, it shows relative payment levels against your uniform charges, useful context for contracting
- **Fee schedule review:** an unusually high gross rate can mean charges sit below some contracted allowables — leaving money on the table with payers that would pay more

## The Fee Schedule Connection

Charges should exceed every contracted allowable (billing below an allowable forfeits the difference with most payers), which is why practices set fees as multiples of Medicare rates. The gross rate mechanically reflects that multiple. Reviewing fee schedules periodically against contracts and current reference rates is the real action item hiding behind this metric.

## Common Errors

- Grading billing performance on gross rate across practices or vendors
- Reacting to gross-rate changes without checking whether the fee schedule moved
- Setting charges so low that some contracts pay billed-charge amounts below allowables
- Reporting gross rate without net collection rate beside it

## Practical Checklist

- [ ] Net collection rate reported as the performance metric; gross rate as context
- [ ] Fee schedule changes annotated on any gross-rate trend
- [ ] Per-payer gross rates reviewed for contracting context annually
- [ ] Charge master verified above all contracted allowables

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is a good gross collection rate?**
There is no meaningful universal benchmark — the number is a function of your markup. On a stable fee schedule, your own trend is informative; against other practices, the comparison mostly measures whose charges are higher.

**Why report it at all?**
As context: paired with net collection rate it helps explain payer mix and fee-schedule dynamics, and its trend on stable charges is a cheap early-warning signal. Alone, it answers questions nobody should be asking.
